The through line

Getting a model to work is the easy part

A model that scores well in a notebook is a starting point, not a finished product. The real work, and the reason enterprises buy a platform, is everything between "it runs on my laptop" and "we can defend this to a regulator in two years."

  • A demo is one run. Production is thousands of runs, across many people, changing data, and time.
  • The hard parts are not the math. They are trust, accountability, and repeatability.
  • These four problems are where data science turns into engineering.

01

Model governance

From a 2011 banking letter to autonomous agents, in fifteen years.

Model governance

Governing a model means governing its whole life

  • An inventory: every model in use, what it does, who owns it, what it touches.
  • A lifecycle: development, validation, approval, monitoring, and retirement.
  • Independent review: someone other than the builder checks the work.
  • A paper trail: enough evidence to reconstruct any decision long after the fact.

In financial services this discipline has a name: model risk management.

The rules

Every regulated industry has a rulebook for models

Finance

SR 11-7, the US model risk rule from 2011, refreshed as SR 26-2 in 2026. TL;DR: prove your models are sound, and have someone independent check them.

Life sciences

21 CFR Part 11 and GAMP 5. TL;DR: electronic records and signatures must be trustworthy, and software has to be validated before it touches a study.

Public sector

NIST AI RMF. TL;DR: a US playbook for governing AI risk, used where there is no single binding law.

EU AI Act

The broad cross industry one. TL;DR: AI is sorted by risk, and high risk systems carry strict requirements.

The catch: the rules lag the technology. SR 26-2 even puts generative and agentic AI out of scope. Governance is a moving target.

The hard part

Most governance lives on an org chart, not in the system

In 2026 surveys, teams could draw a clean governance structure but had not built the controls underneath it. Governance that is only a document gets skipped under deadline pressure. Governance built into the platform holds up in production.

Versioning

Every artifact pinned and recoverable.

Access and approval

Who can promote a model, and on whose sign off.

Audit logs

An automatic record, not a manual one.

The aim is governance that is part of the system, so it still holds when a deadline hits.

02

Reproducibility

If you cannot reproduce it, you cannot govern it, explain it, or audit it.

Reproducibility

Versioning your code is not enough

In ordinary software, the same code gives the same build. In machine learning the result depends on three moving things at once. Change any one of them and the model changes.

Data

The training set

Its contents, its distribution, and every preprocessing step.

Model

The algorithm

Architecture, hyperparameters (the training settings), features, and random seeds.

Environment

The machine

Library versions, dependencies, and even the specific GPU.

Reproducibility, the deep end

Sometimes the same code does not even give the same answer

  • Nondeterminism: a random seed is the starting number for the random steps in training, like how the data is shuffled or how the weights are first set. Leave it unpinned, or let the GPU run its math in a different order, and two identical runs can drift apart.
  • Data leakage: information that would not be available at prediction time sneaks into training, so the model looks excellent in testing and then fails on real data. Kapoor and Narayanan found this across 17 scientific fields and at least 294 papers.
  • The fix is boring and dependable: capture everything. Version the data, the code, and the environment together, log every experiment, and make any past result rebuildable later.

Everything else, governance, explanation, and audit, depends on getting this part right.

03

Explainability

Can you say why, in a way a person, an auditor, or a regulator will accept?

Explainability

There is no single way to explain a model

Post hoc

Explain from the outside

Probe a finished black box through its inputs and outputs. SHAP and LIME are the two standard tools, covered next.

Intrinsic

Use a legible model

A glassbox that is readable by design. Sometimes the right move is a simpler model, not a better excuse for a complex one.

Mechanistic

Reverse engineer it

Trace the internal computation itself. The research frontier, and still labor intensive.

Explainability, pick the right tool

Explaining a model depends on what kind of model it is

Tabular and tree models

SHAP and LIME still work, and auditors still expect them. Feature attribution that sums to the prediction. This covers most regulated decisioning: credit, claims, risk scores.

Large language models

The post hoc playbook breaks. Too many parameters, correlated tokens, noisy token attribution. A different toolkit, covered next.

The mistake is using one method everywhere. Match the method to the model.

Explainability, the two workhorses

Where SHAP and LIME came from

By the mid 2010s the most accurate models were also the least readable. Two methods became the standard way to ask a black box why it made one particular prediction.

LIME, 2016

From Ribeiro, Singh, and Guestrin, in a paper called "Why should I trust you?" To explain one prediction, it nudges the inputs, watches how the output moves, and fits a simple, readable model to that local behavior. It works on any model.

SHAP, 2017

From Lundberg and Lee. It borrows Shapley values, a 1950s idea from game theory for splitting a shared payout fairly among players. Treat each input as a player and split the credit for the prediction. More consistent than LIME, but slower.

Both answer "which inputs mattered for this one prediction," not "how does the model work in general."

Explainability, a quick primer

What is actually inside a large model

  • The basic unit is a neuron: it takes some numbers in, multiplies each by a learned weight, adds them up, and passes the result on. Stack millions of these in layers and you have a neural network.
  • Those weights are called parameters, and they are what the model learns during training. A large language model has billions of them, arranged in a structure called a transformer.
  • You would like to point at one neuron and say "this is the fraud detector." It rarely works that way: one concept is spread across many neurons, and one neuron takes part in many concepts.

That spread out, overlapping storage is exactly what makes a large model hard to read.

Explainability, the LLM toolkit

For a large language model the methods change

  • Mechanistic interpretability: pull internal activations apart into readable features with sparse autoencoders, then trace and even steer them. The research frontier, TransformerLens is the common library.
  • Attention and gradient attribution: which tokens moved the output. Useful, but only a partial picture.
  • Reasoning traces: read the chain of thought the model wrote. Easy to get, but see the next slide.
  • The runtime layer: score groundedness and citation faithfulness, and trace every step. For agents this is what explainability looks like in production.

This is the same tracing and observability idea from the engineering bridge, applied to models.

Explainability, the hard part

The tools have limits, and the models keep getting harder to read

  • LIME can be unstable: probe the model a little differently and the explanation changes.
  • Post hoc tools do not scale to large models: running SHAP over a model with billions of parameters is not practical. SHAP and LIME were built for smaller, spreadsheet style data of rows and columns.
  • That overlap from the last slide has a name, polysemanticity, and it is why you cannot just read a single neuron. Sparse autoencoders are a newer tool that tries to untangle the mess into features you can actually read.
  • The interpretability illusion: an explanation that looks safe while the model quietly leans on a biased proxy, a stand in feature like a site or source ID that secretly encodes something you did not intend.

Meanwhile the pressure rises: the EU AI Act expects explanation for high risk systems.

Explainability, the catch

A reason that sounds right is not always the real one

You can ask a model to explain itself, and it will. The problem is faithfulness: the explanation can be plausible and still not match what actually drove the answer.

What you get

A fluent rationale, or a chain of thought that reads like reasoning.

What you cannot assume

That it is the true cause. Studies show models often justify answers after the fact, sometimes for the wrong reasons.

Treat a model's stated reasoning as a claim to verify, not as evidence. There is even a paper titled "Large language models cannot explain themselves."

04

Bias and blind spots

A model is only as good as the data behind it, and the data is never the whole world.

Bias and blind spots

Models inherit the limits of their data

  • A model learns the data you give it, including its gaps. If the data does not represent the real world, the model is confidently wrong exactly where it was never taught.
  • Trading: a model trained on calm markets can misread a crisis, because the rare events that matter most are the rarest in the data.
  • Life sciences: a model built on one patient population may not transfer to another, and the underrepresented group gets the worst predictions.
  • Signals intelligence: a detector tuned to known conditions can miss a novel tactic or an environment it never saw.

The dangerous part: a model with a blind spot can still score well on a test set that shares the same blind spot. You need explainability and monitoring to catch it.

The whole picture

Not four problems. One problem, four ways.

Governance, the wrapper

The accountability and evidence around everything below. When a regulator asks, the rest becomes governance evidence.

Explainability

Feeds bias detection. You cannot fix what you cannot see.

Bias and blind spots

Needs explanation and monitoring to catch.

Monitoring

Catches drift while it is still small.

Reproducibility, the floor

No reproducibility means no real audit, no trustworthy explanation, and no way to check where the model is blind.

Handled separately, they tend to break. They hold up when one system handles all four at once.

What makes this interesting

Four reasons these problems resist easy answers

Randomness

These systems have randomness built in, so "the same result" is itself a moving target.

Judgment

What counts as good enough, representative enough, or explained enough is a human call.

Scale

A person can audit one decision. Production makes millions, continuously.

A moving target

The technology keeps outrunning the rulebook.

For the engineers in the room

You have solved versions of all four before

  • Reproducibility is version control plus environments. Git pins your code. Here you also pin the data and the exact libraries, all as one unit.
  • Governance is access control and sign offs: who can push a model to production, and who approved it. The access piece is often called RBAC, role based access control.
  • Explainability is tracing. A feature attribution is basically a trace for "why did this prediction come out this way."
  • Bias and blind spots is testing, except the target keeps moving, because someone first has to decide what counts as correct.
  • Monitoring is being on call for models. Same idea as site reliability engineering, or SRE, where you watch a running service and get paged when it breaks. A model breaks quietly, with no error thrown.

Nobody wants to hand build those five per customer. That is what a platform is for.
